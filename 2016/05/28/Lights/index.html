Lights

Veröffentlicht am 2016-05-28

All the wisdom of Henri Cartier-Bresson and others seems too good to be true but I do believe that the purpose of photography is to tell stories. These chosen images featuring UofT’s signature buildings with the mounted or embedded windowpanes, from the Romanesque Revival University College, to KPMB’s latest modernism masterpiece, the Rotman Central, unequivocally make us wonder what was, is and will be behind them. What would the faces look like? What were the vivid, vague or dying memories of UofT undergrads, grads and alumni that had worked in these rooms?

I deliberately selected these night shots, trying to uncloak the realities that lurk beneath the seemingly calm surface. The light has been there for tens of thousands of nights, now joyous, now melancholy, sometimes flickering, sometimes austere, but always tranquil and uncompromisingly sacred. These images are not meant to take the Herculean task of showcasing an intriguing piece of UofT history, but I hope viewers of these images could feel, along with me, all the loves and hates, joy and sufferings, dreams and struggles behind these windows. As Charles Baudelaire once wrote, “Looking from outside into an open window one never sees as much as when one looks through a closed window. There is nothing more profound, more mysterious, more pregnant, more insidious, more dazzling than a window lighted by a single candle. What one can see out in the sunlight is always less interesting than what goes on behind a windowpane. In that black or luminous square life lives, life dreams, life suffers.”
